## 1. Investing & Capital Allocation

### Core Philosophy

- **What matters is how much you make when you're right, not how often you're right.** Frequency of correctness is less important than magnitude of payoff. Asymmetric bets win long-term.
  — Taleb, *Skin in the Game*

- **An investment is one that, after thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and a satisfactory return. Everything else is speculation.** Three-step analysis: descriptive, critical, selective.
  — Graham via Hagstrom, *Warren Buffett: Sein Weg*

- **Inertia often outperforms activity.** Buy-and-hold frequently beats frequent trading. Active management tends to detract from performance.
  — Nicolai Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

- **Prioritize avoiding embarrassing losses over maximizing gains.** Losses punch harder than gains (loss aversion is real, use it as a feature not a bug).
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*; Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

- **Expectations, not cash flows, drive valuations.** The market prices what it expects, not what currently is.
  — Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

### Valuation & Analysis

- **Read the notes before the headlines.** Critical risks hide in financial statement footnotes, not press releases.
  — Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

- **EBITDA has limitations.** It obscures capital intensity, stock comp dilution, and maintenance capex. Use it as a starting point, never the endpoint.
  — Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

- **Buybacks can mask stagnant earnings.** Per-share metrics improve while the business flat-lines. Check absolute numbers.
  — Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

- **Circular capital creates fragility.** When companies fund growth by issuing equity that's used to buy back stock or fund acquisitions that issue more equity — question the loop.
  — Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

- **Markets misprice persistent high growth.** Public markets systematically underprice companies sustaining >30% growth because it's unnatural to model multi-year compounding. Investing in persistent growers at reasonable multiples can be lower risk than low-growth buyouts.
  — David George, *Invest Like the Best*

### Risk Management

- **Use sports psychology to maintain consistent risk-taking after losses.** Debrief systematically to prevent loss aversion from reducing your risk appetite when you need it most.
  — Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

- **Cheap risk is dangerous.** When risk feels cheap (low rates, easy credit), everyone crowds in, making the eventual unwind severe.
  — Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

- **Structure determines investor behavior.** Fee structures and leverage incentives shape manager behavior more than stated intentions. Watch alignment.
  — *The Weekly Wrap*

- **Natural governors smooth cycles.** Constraints like TSMC capacity and power availability prevent unchecked overbuilding and keep growth orderly.
  — Gavin Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

### Focus Investing

- **Two things matter: competence of management and the nature of the business.** Everything else is secondary.
  — Hagstrom, *Warren Buffett: Sein Weg*

- **If you're young, the biggest investment lesson is figuring out who you really are.**
  — Hagstrom, *Warren Buffett: Sein Weg*

- **Noah didn't start building the ark when it was already raining.**
  — Buffett via Hagstrom

## 2. Strategy & Competition

### First Principles

- **Competitive markets destroy profits.** If you're in a competitive market, you're fighting over scraps. Seek monopoly-like advantages.
  — Thiel, *Zero to One*

- **It is better to risk boldness than triviality. A bad plan is better than no plan. Sales matters just as much as product.**
  — Thiel, *Zero to One*

- **Start with a small market you can dominate, then expand.** Don't try to create a new market prematurely — but also don't just compete on existing terms.
  — Thiel, *Zero to One*

- **Conventional wisdom: fail conventionally rather than succeed unconventionally.** Keynes observed that reputation punishes unconventional success less than unconventional failure. Recognize this bias and push past it when evidence supports you.
  — Keynes via Hagstrom

### Game Theory & Strategic Thinking

- **Three-step thinking: what happens next, what happens after that, what are the unintended consequences.** Most people stop at step one.
  — *Three Steps Ahead*

- **Analyse as a three-stage process: descriptive (what is), critical (what's wrong), selective (what to do).**
  — Graham via Hagstrom

### Competitive Advantage

- **How startups outrun incumbents: business-model shifts, new UI/UX, and novel data sources.** Not better execution of the same thing.
  — David George, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Pull beats push.** Test if the market is actively demanding more of your product. Pull businesses scale far easier than pushed ones.
  — David George, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Prefer early product cycles over cheap capital.** The ideal environment pairs early product adoption with a weak capital cycle.
  — David George, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Foundation models without unique data and distribution are the fastest depreciating assets.**
  — Gavin Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

### Power & Positioning

- **Manage the essentials, influentials, and interchangeables.** Every leader must understand who keeps them in power and allocate accordingly.
  — *The Dictator's Handbook*

- **90% of technological surplus goes to end users, not vendors.** Plan your capture strategy accordingly.
  — David George, *Invest Like the Best*

## 3. Leadership & People

### Decision-Making

- **Delay high-stakes decisions when under emotional stress.** Stress rewires thinking and distorts probability assessment.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

- **Hold beliefs loosely AND stubbornly.** Be stubborn enough for contrarian convictions but willing to change when evidence demands it.
  — Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

- **Break consistency traps.** Once committed to a position, people irrationally defend it. Regularly re-examine commitments from scratch.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

- **Always give the why.** People comply better and make better autonomous decisions when they understand the reasoning.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

- **AI gives information, not insight.** Master basics before tools. Tools without fundamentals mean you can't diagnose when automation fails.
  — Scilipoti, *Knowledge Project*

### Managing Teams

- **Ritualize dissent with physical tokens.** Use depersonalized mechanisms (like passing a puck) to encourage constructive disagreement without social friction.
  — Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

- **Protect makers from noise.** Shield creative and technical workers from interruptions and organizational overhead.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

- **Fix incentives, fix performance.** If people aren't performing, check the incentive structure before blaming individuals. "People, Service, Profit" — in that order.
  — Fred Smith, *FedEx Outliers*

- **Permit successors to fail.** Let successors learn by doing and allow controlled failures when transitioning leadership.
  — Ari Emanuel, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Do 140+ conversations before starting a new role.** Triangulate priorities through broad input before acting.
  — Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

- **Lead change as a united front.** Prioritize few initiatives. Over-communicate.
  — Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

### Hiring & Talent

- **Back technical founders who learn business.** Prioritize "technical terminators" who can evolve into great CEOs.
  — David George, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Judge people by track record, not words.** Past behavior is the best predictor.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

## 4. Execution & Habits

### Systems Over Goals

- **Without good financial habits, you will always be struggling for the next dollar.** The people without their habits handled have the least freedom.
  — Clear, *Atomic Habits*

- **Pointing-and-Calling reduces errors by 85% and accidents by 30%.** Make the implicit explicit. Name what you're doing out loud.
  — Clear, *Atomic Habits*

- **The single most important thing during a workday is making progress in meaningful work.** Progress is the primary motivational force.
  — Baker, *The 1% Rule*

- **Don't use personal development as an escape from execution.** Seminars and frameworks are tools to execute, not substitutes for it.
  — Baker, *The 1% Rule*

### Speed & Urgency

- **Treat speed as a mindset.** Answer quickly, keep communications short, build urgency into tasks.
  — Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

- **Think big and move fast.** Speed and ambition as competitive advantage. Scalability should be a filter for every business decision.
  — Brad Jacobs, *Invest Like the Best*

### Clarity

- **Write crisp, explicit specifications.** Whether for people or AI agents, invest upfront in clear specs to get reliable results.
  — Jeff Dean, *Latent Space*

- **Draft first, then edit.** Separate creation from evaluation. Apply broadly to writing, strategy, and product development.
  — *Life Advice from Legendary Writers*

## 5. Persuasion & Communication

### Influence Mechanics

- **We like people we trust, and we're more convinced by people we like.** Likability is underrated as a persuasion tool.
  — *Knowledge Project Best of 2025*

- **Reciprocation is the most powerful influence tool.** Use it wisely — give first, genuinely.
  — Cialdini, *Influence*; Munger

- **Hatred and strong emotions distort factual assessment.** Account for this in yourself and in others you negotiate with.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

- **Question social proof.** Just because everyone does it doesn't make it right. Social proof is strongest (and most dangerous) in ambiguous situations.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

- **Avoid contrast misdirection.** A $50 accessory feels cheap after a $5,000 purchase. Watch for this in negotiations and presentations.
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

### Communication Style

- **Write plain, conversational copy.** Convert features into benefits. Prefer active verbs and plain Anglo-Saxon words.
  — Sutherland, *Knowledge Project*

- **Solve for feelings, not just numbers.** Define objectives with psychology, not just metrics. Consider changing perceptions before changing technology.
  — Sutherland, *Knowledge Project*

- **Dealmaking is a dance.** Both sides must feel good. Charm and timing matter more than raw aggression.
  — Ari Emanuel, *Invest Like the Best*

### Marketing & Positioning

- **Marketing is fat-tailed.** 5-10% of what you do delivers 110% of the value. Hourly evaluation underfunds rare campaigns that deliver outsized returns.
  — Sutherland, *Knowledge Project*

- **Innovation needs MORE marketing, not less.** New ideas require more explanation. The best technology doesn't always win.
  — Sutherland, *Knowledge Project*

- **Design beats specs at scale.** Small design details multiplied across billions of uses outweigh superior engineering.
  — Sutherland, *Knowledge Project*

## 6. Mental Models & Biases

### Cognitive Biases (Munger's Framework)

- **Biases combine explosively.** A single bias is manageable; multiple concurrent biases (social proof + authority + commitment) create catastrophic misjudgment. Always check: how many biases are active right now?
  — Munger, *Psychology of Human Misjudgement*

- **Confirmation bias is the default.** Actively seek disconfirming evidence.
  — Munger

- **Availability bias:** what comes easily to mind feels more probable. Counteract with base rates.
  — Munger

- **Envy drives more conflict than greed.** Manage it in organizations by making reward systems transparent and fair.
  — Munger

- **Influence through mere association.** People transfer feelings from messengers to messages. Choose your associations carefully.
  — Munger

- **Practice rare skills often.** Skills you rarely use decay fastest. Simulate edge cases regularly.
  — Munger

### Thinking Tools

- **Memorize rough system constants.** Having mental models of latencies, costs, and resource numbers enables quick back-of-the-envelope decisions.
  — Jeff Dean, *Latent Space*

- **Curiosity is a defensive tool.** It counteracts overconfidence and ideology. Stay genuinely curious.
  — Munger

- **Trust objective reasoning over social pressure.** When the crowd disagrees with your analysis, verify the analysis — but don't default to the crowd.
  — Wozniak, *Outliers*

- **Do the probability math.** Don't rely on intuition for probabilities. Actually calculate.
  — Munger

### Asymmetries

- **Skin in the game reveals truth.** People who bear consequences of their decisions make better decisions. Distrust advice from those without downside.
  — Taleb, *Skin in the Game*

- **You want to win, not win arguments.** Optimize for outcomes, not for being right in the conversation.
  — Taleb, *Skin in the Game*

- **Have no assistant (Vardi principle).** The mere presence of an assistant suspends your natural filtering. Its absence forces you toward only things you genuinely want to do.
  — Taleb, *Skin in the Game*

## 7. Entrepreneurship & Building

### Starting Up

- **A startup is the largest group of people you can convince of a plan to build a different future.**
  — Thiel, *Zero to One*

- **Getting comfortable being uncomfortable and failing builds entrepreneurial success.** Prior failures are the training ground.
  — Finkelstein, *Knowledge Project*

- **Thorough due diligence before acquisitions.** Use a quadrant framework for M&A: categorize by strategic fit and financial attractiveness.
  — Brad Jacobs, *Invest Like the Best*

### Scaling

- **Information is as valuable as the physical product.** FedEx realized tracking was as important as delivery. Applies broadly: data layer > service layer.
  — Fred Smith, *FedEx Outliers*

- **Creators become their own networks.** Creators and podcasters will become distribution platforms and brand owners. Build distribution as an asset.
  — Ari Emanuel, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Cultural integration determines M&A success.** Merging cultures post-acquisition is the hardest part. Plan for it explicitly.
  — Brad Jacobs, *Invest Like the Best*

### Knowing When to Retreat

- **Retreat when reality changes.** FedEx killed Zapmail (a costly misread of fax technology). Cut losses when the thesis breaks.
  — Fred Smith, *FedEx Outliers*

- **Misjudging a trend is expensive.** Recognize trend-judgment errors early. The cost of stubbornness compounds.
  — Brad Jacobs, *Invest Like the Best*

## 8. Technology & AI (Current Cycle)

### Scaling & Architecture

- **Pre-training scaling laws still hold.** No one knows how or why, but they've been measured extremely precisely. Don't bet against them.
  — Gavin Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Anything you can verify, you can automate.** (Karpathy) This is a more precise framing than "anything you can specify."
  — Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Reasoning bridged an 18-month hardware gap.** Post-training scaling laws (reasoning, RL) created progress when hardware plateaued.
  — Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Cheaper tokens enable longer thinking.** Lower per-token cost lets models reason more, unlocking capabilities that were cost-prohibitive.
  — Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Sparsity unlocks massive capacity.** Trillions of parameters with a small active subset. Outrageously large models, low activation cost.
  — Jeff Dean, *Latent Space*

### Building with AI

- **SaaS must embrace low-margin agents.** Trying to preserve 80% gross margins guarantees failure in AI. Accept lower margins for higher-value outcomes.
  — Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Reasoning creates a data flywheel.** Product usage produces verifiable signals that improve models. Ship to learn.
  — Baker, *Invest Like the Best*

- **Unified models outcompete specialized ones.** One multimodal model increasingly replaces many task-specific systems.
  — Jeff Dean, *Latent Space*

- **Build the SDK first so humans and agents share it.** SDK-first approach makes both human and agentic workflows reliable.
  — Singleton, *Latent Space*

- **Tools matter more than agent wrappers.** Invest in robust tool interfaces, not framework layers.
  — Singleton, *Latent Space*

### Infrastructure

- **Power is the critical constraint.** 100k GPUs requires ~150MW. Grid expansion can't keep pace. Data centers are becoming their own private utilities.
  — Lapukhov, *Latent Space*; *Aligned on Powering Data Centers*

- **CoreWeave: from $16M to $5.3B revenue in 3 years.** GPU-focused infrastructure is a viable hyperscale play when backed by long contracts.
  — *Data Center Boom*

- **Value by unit economics, not EBITDA multiples** for GPU infrastructure businesses.
  — *Data Center Boom*

  — Taleb, *Skin in the Game*

  — Taleb, *Skin in the Game*

- **High ambitions are fantastic: you achieve great things even if you fail. Low ambitions achieve nothing even if you succeed.**

  — von Freyhold / Tangen, *Knowledge Project*

  — Sutherland, *Knowledge Project*

  — Ari Emanuel, *Invest Like the Best*

  — Brad Jacobs, *Invest Like the Best*
